Why the psychology of sustainability is the defining challenge of our era

We know what is happening to our planet. We have the technologies, the policies, the data. And yet, collective action remains elusive. The gap between knowing and doing, between caring abstractly and acting concretely, is fundamentally a psychological problem.

My research program sits at the intersection of moral psychology, environmental science, and education. Housed in Arizona State University's School of Sustainability within the College of Global Futures, I ask: What psychological mechanisms allow some people — and some communities — to genuinely care about and act on behalf of a future they will never see? And how do we cultivate those mechanisms at scale?

Drawing on over 92 peer-reviewed publications in outlets including PNAS, Nature Reviews Psychology, Nature Human Behaviour, and Science Advances, I have developed a coherent, evidence-based research program centered on four interconnected pillars — each representing both a scientific frontier and a high-leverage investment opportunity for private funders who want their philanthropy to move the needle on the sustainability crisis.

Futures Thinking

Training minds to care across time

Most people care deeply about their children. Far fewer extend that care to strangers alive in 2150. My research asks: why? And more importantly — can we change this?

Using large-scale behavioral experiments, cross-national surveys, and intervention science, I have shown that targeted educational exercises — "longtermist" training, intergenerational perspective-taking, seventh-generation framing — measurably increase people's sense of moral responsibility for future generations and translate directly into pro-climate action.

This pillar asks: What curricula, games, immersive experiences, and policy nudges most reliably cultivate futures-oriented thinking? How can we bring these to schools, workplaces, and civic institutions?

The Clergy Effect

Unlocking faith communities for sustainability

Religious congregations represent one of the largest and most trusted community networks in the world — yet they have been largely overlooked by the sustainability movement.

In a landmark study published in PNAS (2025), I found that the majority of American religious leaders privately believe in human-caused climate change — yet rarely discuss it with their congregations. More importantly, informing congregants of their leader's beliefs significantly increases climate concern and openness to dialogue, particularly among politically conservative audiences who are hardest to reach through conventional channels.

This pillar leverages religious institutions as an underutilized high-trust channel for sustainability messaging, intergenerational ethics, and community-based environmental action — partnering clergy with conservation organizations, policymakers, and educators.

Sustainable Education

Cultivating sustainability as a virtue

Knowledge alone does not produce sustainable behavior. Virtue does. Drawing on character psychology and virtue ethics, this pillar asks how we can train sustainability not as a set of facts or even habits, but as a deeply held character orientation — something people are, not just something they do.

Through collaborations including a $1.6M John Templeton Foundation Character Virtue Development Grant, I am developing and testing experiential curricula — including farm-based learning, ecological humility interventions, and virtue-centered coursework at ASU — that build lasting pro-sustainability character traits in students and early-career professionals.

The vision: a workforce that doesn't just comply with sustainability requirements but embraces them as core to their identity.

Nature Connectedness

Rebuilding the human–nature bond

We cannot protect what we do not love. In an age of screens and urban environments, many people experience profound disconnection from the natural world — and this disconnection is a direct predictor of reduced pro-environmental behavior.

My research investigates the psychological pathways through which people come to feel genuinely connected to nature. Published work spanning 76 countries shows that nature connectedness is a robust predictor of well-being and ecological concern. I am developing and testing scalable interventions — including nature-based education programs, contemplative practices, and urban design recommendations — to restore this bond in populations with limited access to natural environments.

Alongside this, I conducted the first psychological study of Ecological Humility — a distinct moral orientation toward the natural world that predicts responsible environmental stewardship above and beyond standard measures of environmental concern.
